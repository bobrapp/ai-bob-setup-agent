Bob Rapp Launches ai-bob-setup-agent: One-Click Onboarding for AI Employees

Solo AI agency now provisions enterprise-grade agents in fifteen minutes — the same setup that took an afternoon last quarter.

ELLENSBURG, WASHINGTON — May 2026. Bob Rapp today announced ai-bob-setup-agent, an automated onboarding system that provisions Hermes and OpenClaw AI agents end-to-end for legacy SMB customers. The system reduces the manual setup time per customer from roughly six hours to under fifteen minutes, removing the single biggest bottleneck in scaling a one-person AI services business.

"Every operator in this space has the same problem," said Rapp. "Sales is easy — 99% of businesses know they're behind. Setup is hard. Every customer needs the same ritual: workspace, cloud computers, runtime install, MCPs, watchdogs, Telegram channel. ai-bob-setup-agent runs that ritual for me. I sign a customer at noon, their agents are alive by twelve-fifteen."

Rapp's first three customers are in marketing, commercial real estate, and matrimonial law — each paying between $5,000 and $10,000 per month for unlimited agent usage. None of the three have an in-house technical team. None have been asked to think about tokens, credits, or API limits. "The price is the deal," Rapp said. "Unlimited everything, no meter, no surprise invoices. That's the offer."

The repository is open-source under MIT license at github.com/bobrapp/ai-bob-setup-agent.

Customer FAQ

What am I actually buying?

An AI employee. Or two. Or three. They do the work you described in our discovery call — outbound prospecting, document drafting, client follow-up, reporting — and they don't quit, don't get sick, and don't need a 401k. You get a Telegram channel where you can talk to them or to me. Monthly contract, 30-day mutual out.

How long until they're working?

Fifteen minutes from contract signed. We discovered your use case on the first call; the second call is the agents going live.

What if I need more agents next month?

Add them. The price doesn't move. "Unlimited" means unlimited. Most customers settle into one to three agents that handle the bulk of the work; we can spin up extras whenever you want.

What if it breaks?

Our watchdog catches agent failures within a minute and pages me on Telegram before you notice. The whole reason this is a $5K–$10K product and not a $500 product is that uptime and reliability are priced in. You don't run the infrastructure; we do.

Can I see what the agent is doing?

Yes. Daily digest email summarizing every task. Real-time alerts on anything material. Full action log if you ever want to audit. You can also just ask the agent — it knows what it did this week.

Why not hire a junior employee instead?

Different cost structure. A junior BDR is $60K base plus benefits plus management overhead, ramps for six months, and might leave in eighteen. The Hermes tier is $120K/year, doesn't ramp, doesn't leave, and you can cancel in thirty days. That's the comparison.

What about data privacy?

Per-customer isolated workspace. Your data does not cross into other customers' agents. Standard model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic) under their respective enterprise terms. We're not currently a fit for HIPAA or PCI environments — talk to us in 2027.

Executive FAQ

What's the gross margin?

Approximately 91% on both tiers. COGS is roughly $440 (OpenClaw) or $880 (Hermes) per customer per month, dominated by model spend. Per-customer compute on Orgo is modest because most agents are I/O-bound, not CPU-bound.

Where does revenue come from in the first 90 days?

Two paid pilots converted from existing professional network. One marketing agency, one mid-size manufacturer. Each at the OpenClaw tier as a hedge against my own delivery risk. Upgrade conversation at month four.

What's the CAC story?

We're betting on content as the primary acquisition channel. Weekly LinkedIn + Loom on a specific automation with a specific outcome. CAC for the first ten customers will look more like $300 of light ad spend plus four hours of conversation per customer. Anything more aggressive is reserved for post-month-six when product-market fit is proven.

What's the path from $900K to $5M?

Three possible motions. (1) Hire delivery support so I can keep selling — likely. (2) White-label the setup agent to other solo operators who want a turnkey AI services business — possible. (3) Productize a specific vertical agent (e.g. "matrimonial law AI assistant") and sell it self-serve — opportunistic.

Why is this defensible?

Three layers, in increasing strength. Speed — a solo operator with a strong setup agent ships customer-specific changes in hours, not sprints. Sub-niche brand — "AI for matrimonial law in NY" is a category of one. Compounding operational data — every customer makes the setup agent better at provisioning and the watchdog better at remediation.

What kills this?

Three things. Model price collapse compressing the "unlimited" framing's value. A big-platform bundling agents into existing SaaS suites. Operator burnout. Mitigations exist for all three — see the business plan's Risks section.

Common objections

?
"$10K/month is a lot."

It's less than a junior employee, doesn't ramp, doesn't churn, doesn't need management. The "unlimited" framing means it doesn't scale with usage either. Customers who do the math on hours-saved get there quickly.

?
"Why not wait for ChatGPT to do this?"

ChatGPT doesn't run inside your law firm's intake workflow, doesn't have an email address that signs off as Sarah-from-the-firm, and doesn't get reset by my watchdog at 3 AM. The platform is generic; this is a deployed solution.

?
"What if you get hit by a bus?"

Fair. The code is open-source on GitHub. The Telegram meta-agent is the operational interface — another competent operator can take it over with the README. We're also documenting a "successor playbook" in docs/ as customer count grows.

Internal release note

v1
ai-bob-setup-agent v0.1.0 shipped

Idempotent CLI for Orgo + Hermes/OpenClaw provisioning. 13 smoke tests green. CI runs lint + tests + dry-run onboard on every push. Pages deploys docs site automatically.

v0.1.0 is the first cut of the operator-facing tooling that turns a customer YAML config into a fully-provisioned, monitored agent fleet. The CLI is Click-based, the orchestration uses structlog for structured logs, and the config layer is Pydantic-validated. All third-party API surfaces (Orgo, Hermes, Telegram, SMTP) are dependency-injected at the entry points so unit tests run without network access.

Three commands cover the operator's day: onboard, add-agent, decommission. The healthcheck and watchdog scripts give you one-shot and long-running observability respectively.

Technical FAQ

What's the dependency surface?

Python 3.10+. pydantic, pyyaml, python-dotenv, click, rich, httpx, structlog, python-telegram-bot, aiosmtplib. Dev adds ruff and pytest. No Node, no Docker, no system packages beyond Python and curl.

Is everything idempotent?

Yes. ensure_workspace looks up by slug before creating. ensure_cloud_computer lists existing computers in the workspace and returns the match if found. MCP install is keyed by package name. Re-runs of make onboard on an existing customer converge — they don't duplicate or recreate.

How do you handle secrets?

.env file at repo root, gitignored. python-dotenv loads on demand. Customer YAMLs live under config/customers/ which is also gitignored — the example template is the only YAML in version control. Secrets never enter logs (structured logs include a key-presence boolean but never the key itself).

What's the test strategy?

Smoke tests validate config parsing, stack file presence, pricing invariants from the source video, dry-run idempotency on the Orgo client, MCP registry completeness, and a full dry-run onboard that walks the entire provisioning path without network calls. CI runs these plus a lint pass and an actual dry-run of the CLI.

How does the dry-run mode work?

OrgoClient(dry_run=True) logs intended HTTP calls and returns stub objects with deterministic IDs. HermesInstaller in dry-run logs remote actions but doesn't execute them. The Telegram notifier in dry-run logs the message and returns True. SMTP alerts no-op if config is missing. This means CI can verify the orchestration path without any credentials.

What about real Orgo / Hermes API surfaces?

The HTTP client is wired against documented endpoints (/workspaces, /workspaces/<id>/computers, /computers/<id>/exec). Real paths may need adjustment when you get production keys from Nick's onboarding flow — the abstraction is intentionally thin so swaps are cheap. The HermesInstaller's _run_remote method is the explicit extension point for executing remote shell on a cloud computer once the exec API is wired.

Logging?

Structured JSON via structlog. Every action emits an event with a stable event name and structured fields. Easy to ship to a log aggregator or grep locally. Operational provenance also appended to docs/build-log.md per the AIGovOps Foundation rule.

Architecture FAQ

Why a workspace per customer?

Blast-radius isolation. A customer's runaway agent or compromised MCP shouldn't be able to touch another customer's data. Per-workspace Orgo billing also keeps unit-economics legible per customer.

Why cloud computers instead of containers?

Per Nick: cloud computers give agents persistent state, real OS access for tool use, and the ability to actually drive a browser or a desktop app when needed. Containers are great for stateless compute; agents need state. The OS surface area is the moat.

Why Telegram for the meta-agent?

Mobile-first ops. Bob is on a phone half the day. Telegram bot APIs are excellent, command parsing is trivial, and the channel becomes the audit log of every operational action. Slack is also fine — the abstraction in telegram_meta.py wouldn't change much.

Why Obsidian for the second brain?

Per the source video. Markdown-on-disk is durable, diffable, and easy for both agents and humans to work with. No vendor lock-in, no schema migrations, no "the AI's memory company went under." Future versions might add a vector DB as a query accelerator, but the source of truth stays markdown.

Why no Docker?

Operator tooling, not the agent runtime. The setup agent runs on Bob's laptop or a tiny VPS. The agents themselves run on Orgo's cloud computers, which is where the heavy lifting lives. Adding Docker to the operator side adds friction without value.

How do I add a new MCP?

Add a row to MCP_REGISTRY in src/mcp_config.py with the package name and env var. Reference it by name from a customer YAML's agent mcps: list. The installer picks it up automatically.

Common objections

?
"You're going to be locked into Orgo."

The runtime abstraction is in hermes_install.py and the cloud-computer abstraction is in orgo_client.py. Both are under 250 lines. Swapping to a different platform is a focused refactor, not a rewrite.

?
"Why not use Kubernetes / Pulumi / Terraform?"

Heavy machinery for a problem that doesn't need it. We're managing tens of cloud computers across a handful of customers, not thousands of services across regions. The setup agent is roughly 800 lines of Python; that's appropriate scale.

?
"The dry-run mode hides real failures."

Correct — dry-run is for CI and for "show me what would happen." Real failures show up the first time you hit production credentials. The smoke tests assert dry-run behavior; an additional integration test layer runs against real Orgo as soon as the staging key is available.
